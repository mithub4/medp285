Skateboard: Culture, Lifestyle Community & Art!

Skateboarding has come a long way since the 1960s when the first skateboard was invented. What began as a fun activity for surfers to practice their moves on land has evolved into a full-fledged sport and cultural phenomenon. Over the years, skateboarding has undergone significant changes, from the materials used to make boards to the styles of riding and the tricks performed.

Introduction

The origins of skateboard culture and lifestyle can be traced back to Southern California in the 1970s. It was a time when the surf culture was booming, and surfers were looking for a way to practice their moves when the waves were flat. Skateboarding filled that gap, and soon, skateboarders began to develop their own style and culture.

Punk rock music also played a significant role in shaping skateboard culture. Many skateboarders of the time were also punk rock fans, and the DIY ethos of the punk scene found its way into skateboarding. Skateboarders began building their own ramps and obstacles, and the first skateparks were created.

The street art movement of the 1980s also had a significant impact on skateboard culture. Skateboard graphics began to feature more artistic designs, and skateparks became spaces for graffiti artists to showcase their work. The crossover between skateboarding and street art continues to this day, with many skateboarders and artists collaborating on projects.

Today, skateboarding culture and lifestyle have become a global phenomenon, with communities of skateboarders in every corner of the world. Skateboarding has also become more inclusive and diverse, with efforts being made to create a more welcoming and accessible space for everyone, regardless of gender, race, or background.



The Skateboarding Community

One of the defining features of skateboard culture is the sense of community that exists among skateboarders. At its core, skateboarding is a social activity that brings people together from all walks of life, united by their love for the sport and the lifestyle that comes with it.

At the heart of this community are skate shops, local skateparks, and DIY spots. Skate shops are more than just retail stores; they are hubs for the local skateboarding scene, where skaters can buy gear, connect with other riders, and get the latest news and updates on the local skateboarding scene. Many skate shops also host events and competitions, further fostering the sense of community among skateboarders.

Local skateparks are another key element of the skateboarding community. These public spaces provide a safe and accessible place for skaters to practice their skills, connect with other riders, and simply hang out. Skateparks also offer a sense of belonging, with many riders developing strong friendships and connections with fellow skaters who share their passion for the sport.

In recent years, DIY spots have emerged as a key part of the skateboarding community. These spots are typically created by skaters themselves, often in abandoned or unused spaces, and offer a unique and challenging environment for riders. DIY spots can range from small ramps and obstacles to full-blown skateparks, and they often become gathering places for local skateboarders, where they can push their limits and connect with other riders who share their DIY ethos.

One of the most important aspects of the skateboarding community is inclusivity and diversity. Skateboarding has historically been a male-dominated sport, but efforts are being made to create a more welcoming and equitable space for riders of all genders, races, and backgrounds. Many skate shops and skateparks now offer programs and events specifically aimed at promoting diversity and inclusion within the skateboarding community.

Gender representation in skateboarding has also been a hot topic in recent years. While female skateboarders have been a part of the scene for decades, they have often been overlooked and underrepresented in media and competitions. However, this is slowly changing, with more and more female skateboarders breaking through and gaining recognition for their skills and creativity. Organizations such as Girls Skate Network and Skate Like a Girl are also working to promote and support female skateboarders, helping to create a more equitable and welcoming space for all riders.

The sense of community that exists within skateboarding culture is one of its most defining and beloved features. Skate shops, local skateparks, and DIY spots all play a vital role in fostering this community, while inclusivity and diversity are key values that are becoming increasingly important within the skateboarding scene. As skateboarding continues to grow and evolve, it is this sense of community that will keep riders coming back, year after year, to push their limits and connect with other skateboarders who share their passion.



Skateboarding & Art

Skateboarding and street art have a long-standing relationship, with many skaters being heavily influenced by the street art that surrounds them. This intersection of skateboarding and street art has given birth to a unique style and aesthetic that is a hallmark of skateboarding culture. Skatepark murals are one of the most visible forms of skateboarding art, with many skateparks featuring large-scale murals that are both functional and visually stunning. These murals are often created by local artists who are passionate about skateboarding and want to contribute to their local skateboarding community. Skatepark murals can also serve as a way to promote positive messages and inspire young skaters.

Board graphics are another way in which skateboarding and art intersect. Skateboard decks have long been a canvas for artistic expression, with many pro skaters collaborating with artists to create unique and visually striking graphics for their decks. Board graphics can range from simple designs to intricate illustrations, and often reflect the personality and style of the skater who is riding them. Some skateboard companies have even gained a reputation for their artistic and innovative board graphics, such as the iconic Thrasher logo or the Vans checkerboard pattern.

Skateboarding has also had a significant influence on fashion and design. The DIY nature of skateboarding culture has given rise to a unique style that is both functional and stylish. Skaters often wear clothing that is comfortable and durable, such as baggy pants and t-shirts. Skateboarding shoes, such as Vans and Converse, have become synonymous with skateboarding culture and are often worn both on and off the board. Skateboarding fashion has also had an influence on mainstream fashion, with many fashion designers incorporating skateboarding-inspired elements into their designs.

Skateboarding photography and videography have also played a significant role in promoting the artistry of skateboarding. Skateboard photographers and videographers capture the athleticism, style, and creativity of skateboarders, and their work has been instrumental in promoting skateboarding as a legitimate form of art and self-expression. Skateboarding videos, such as the iconic "Yeah Right!" and "Fully Flared," have become cultural touchstones, with their innovative filming techniques and high production values setting a new standard for skateboard filmmaking.

In recent years, there has been a growing interest in the intersection of skateboarding and fine art. Many contemporary artists, such as Shepard Fairey and Barry McGee, have been heavily influenced by skateboarding culture and incorporate skateboarding imagery and iconography into their work. Skateboarding art exhibits and installations have also become more common, with galleries and museums showcasing the unique artistry and cultural significance of skateboarding.

Overall, skateboarding as art is a multifaceted and dynamic aspect of skateboarding culture. From skatepark murals to board graphics, fashion and design, photography and videography, and fine art, skateboarding has had a significant impact on the art world and continues to inspire artists and creatives around the globe. Skateboarding culture celebrates the artistry and creativity of skateboarders and recognizes that skateboarding is not just a sport, but a way of life that encompasses a unique and vibrant culture.
